# Follow-Up Email — Principles

A good follow-up email does four things, in this order:

1. **Acknowledge specifics.** Mention something concrete the recipient said or that the meeting decided. Generic "thanks for your time" reads as a bot. Anchor to one detail the recipient will recognise as theirs.
2. **Restate commitments.** What did each side agree to do, by when? Bullet form. Both directions — what I owe them, what they owe me. Asymmetric lists feel like a checklist *for* the recipient.
3. **Surface one open item.** If there is an unresolved question, name it and say who is taking it forward (and by when). If there is none, omit — manufactured open items add noise.
4. **State the next from your side.** What is the recipient looking out for from you, and when. Without this, the recipient cannot plan.

## What to leave out

- Pleasantries beyond a single line.
- Restating context the recipient already has.
- Vague commitments without a date.
- Multiple asks — pick one.

## What to never invent

- Dates not in the log or just stated by the user.
- Commitments not in the log or just stated by the user.
- Attendee names, titles, or company details not in the entity profile.

If a piece of information is missing and would be required to produce a defensible draft, ask the user before drafting — do not paper over the gap.

## Length

Aim for 80–150 words. A follow-up that runs past 200 words has either too much context or too many asks.
