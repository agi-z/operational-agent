# Follow-Up Email — Tone

The same template produces different emails depending on intent. Tone shifts the register; the structure stays the same.

## By intent

### recap
Most common. Used after a substantive conversation to lock in shared understanding. Tone: collegial, factual, confident. Avoids urgency language. Closing line typically: *"Anything I've missed, just shout."*

### nudge
Used when a date is approaching and a deliverable from one or both sides is still pending, or when a confirmation is needed before the user can move. Tone: friendly but explicit about the timeline. Names the date that creates the urgency rather than asserting urgency abstractly. Closing line typically: *"Let me know if anything's shifted on your end."*

### next-steps
Used after a call where the next phase of work was discussed but not yet committed. Tone: forward-leaning, slightly more directive. Proposes a path; invites pushback. Closing line typically: *"If this lines up, I'll get the [next artifact] over by [date]."*

### intro-response
Used to reply to a cold or warm intro. Tone: warm-but-brief; recipient may not yet know the relationship is worth their time. The anchor must reference the introducer or the channel. Closing line typically: *"Happy to set up a 20-minute call — calendar link below, or just send a time."*

## By relationship stage

- **Early-stage (discovery / first-touch):** lean factual. The recipient is still calibrating whether this relationship is worth their attention; over-familiarity reads as presumption.
- **Mid-cycle (procurement / negotiation):** lean precise. Dates and dollar amounts must be exact; ambiguity costs cycles.
- **Long-standing (renewal / expansion):** can be slightly more casual. Avoid robotic structure; the recipient knows you. But never drop commitments or dates — those still need to be unambiguous.

## What never varies

- A specific anchor in the first paragraph.
- At least one commitment from each side (if any exist).
- A grounded date on every commitment.
- The sender's first name only — no titles, no credentials in the signature.
